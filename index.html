Research

The Ethics of Content Labeling

Social media and the Internet have resulted in an information explosion, and properly dealing with that influx has proved challenging. As COVID-19 has demonstrated, platforms are often ill-equipped to deal with materially damaging misinformation. With an interdisciplinary team under the Ethics Institute, I work alongside an array of researchers and fellow students to deeply analyze and evaluate the different approaches to labeling online content, with insights derived from psychology, computer science, political science, and philosophy.

Specifically within this group, I work with Dr. Briony Swire-Thompson researching the backfire effect, a putative phenomenon by which correcting an individual's mistaken belief produces a paradoxical increase in that individual's belief in the misconception. While the backfire effect has been widely publicized and contributed to social media platforms opting not to remove or correct their misinformation, recent research has had difficulties replicating the effect. Our new research provides a possible explanation for this discrepancy. Using a controlled design with 920 participants, we both failed to replicate the backfire effect and found that backfire effects correlated negatively with measure reliability: the more variance a question had within the same individual, the more likely that question was to produce a backfire effect. This indicates that the backfire effect may be mainly an artifact of measurement errors, a finding which has massive implications for social media platforms and combating misinformation in the modern world.

Scarlet

Scarlet is an open-source Rust library with thousands of downloads. Its goal is to help programmers work with color in a way that matches how humans see color, dealing with concepts like hue, chroma, and saturation without the inaccuracies of common systems like RGB and HSL.

Scarlet lets the programmer simulate what colors appear like in different lighting environments, make gradients, convert between myriad color representations, and more!

FRACTRAN

I once attended a summer camp for mathematics and had the privilege of attending a series of lectures by the great mathematician John Conway (of Game of Life fame). I very fondly remember these lectures, and one in particular I remember quite clearly over half a decade later: his explanation of a programming language of sorts that used fractions and multiplication as its only tool, but that nonetheless could be used to generate prime numbers or anything else other programming languages could.

After his passing from COVID, my mind went back to this lecture, and I thought I would try and implement this language in Rust. This library is the result of that effort. It supports some optimizations that allow you to, within the bounds of the extremely slow nature of the language, run FRACTRAN programs reasonably quickly.
